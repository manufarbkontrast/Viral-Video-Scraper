The water poured into the oceans, altered ocean currents, and pushed massive amounts of moisture into the atmosphere. Over the mammoth steppe, which had always been dry and sunny, heavy clouds formed for the first time. It began to rain.

At the same time, a new army marched from the south: trees. Shrubs, mosses, and the Siberian taiga invaded the steppe. Grass needs direct sunlight and dry soil. For bison and horses, they provided too few nutrients — and could even poison them.

Höhlenlöwen und Säbelzahnkatzen waren zu groß, um im dichten Wald von kleinerer Beute zu leben. Es war kein plötzlicher Tod. When the grass vanished, the dominoes fell. Horses and bison starved or retreated to tiny remnant areas — where humans and cave lions made short work of them. Mammoths and rhinos were pushed into small, isolated populations on islands and in remote valleys. Inbreeding weakened their lineages. When the prey disappeared, so did the predators. Cave lions and saber-toothed cats were too large to survive on smaller prey in the dense forest. It was not a sudden death. It was a process spanning millennia, where every square meter of steppe was devoured by green forest. The last woolly mammoth died on Wrangel Island about 4,000 years ago. At the same time, the Egyptians were building their pyramids. It did not die from lack of water. It died because its empire had become a forest where it could no longer exist.

Editor

20,000 years ago, where London stands today, there was no forest and no ice — but a golden sea of dry grass, wider than all of Europe. The largest ecosystem in Earth's history. And it was not destroyed by an asteroid. It was destroyed by rain.
Most people imagine the Ice Age as a hostile snow desert. The opposite was true. The mammoth steppe stretched from Spain across Siberia to Canada — one continuous strip of land, larger than all modern continents combined. The density of large herbivores exceeded today's Serengeti. Mammoths, woolly rhinos, giant deer, steppe bison — dozens of tons of biomass per square kilometer. This system existed for over 100,000 years. Then it vanished within a few millennia. Not because of the cold, not because of hunters. Because of moisture.
During the last Ice Age, sea levels were 120 meters lower than today — so low that the North Sea and the Bering Strait were dry grassland. The result was an ecological supercontinent stretching uninterrupted from the Iberian Peninsula to Alaska. The reason for this abundance was a climatic paradox: aridity. The massive ice sheets over North America and Northern Europe stripped so much moisture from the atmosphere that clouds barely formed. Without rain, no forests grew. Without thick snow, animals stayed mobile. The snow was so thin that herbivores could push it aside with their hooves.
But this was no ordinary grass. In the cold, dry climate, it dried standing on its roots — like natural hay that retained its nutrients throughout winter. Analyses from the stomachs of mammoth mummies show: this grass contained as much protein and carbohydrates as high-quality grain. The mammoth steppe was a factory that kept itself running. Strong wind prevented snow accumulation. Sunlight powered photosynthesis. And the feet of millions of animals compacted the ground. Below the surface lay permafrost — a frozen layer that released just enough moisture in the brief summer for grass to grow, but not enough for swamps or forests. A fragile balance.
Here is the crucial detail: loose snow insulates the ground. A meter of snow keeps the soil 15 to 20 degrees warmer than the air above — as warm as a heated room compared to the outdoor temperature. Without animals, the permafrost underneath this insulation would have thawed and released methane. But millions of bison and horses trampled the snow flat. Compressed snow loses its insulating effect. Arctic cold of minus 50 degrees penetrated deep into the ground. This kept the permafrost stable. The animals literally protected the foundation of their own empire.
Mammuthus primigenius was not simply an elephant that wandered into the cold. This species is the result of 5 million years of genetic fine-tuning. Their ears were only one-fifteenth the size of an African elephant's — tiny heat-saving windows. A special protein called TRPV3 reduced the skin's sensitivity to cold and promoted dense fur growth. Their hemoglobin carried a mutation that released oxygen to tissues even at temperatures near freezing. Without this mutation, their legs would have died from oxygen deprivation during hours of standing in snow.
Their tusks — up to 4 meters long, spirally curved — were the bulldozers of the Ice Age. Wear marks on fossils show: mammoths did not push them straight forward but performed lateral sweeping motions to break through the ice crust. This was not just vital for their own survival. If this crust remained intact, smaller species like horses and bison starved after a single ice storm. The woolly rhinoceros took on the same role in wind-sheltered valleys where snow piled up thick. Its horn was not made of bone but of keratin — and strangely flat. A specialized snow shovel. Mummified specimens from a Polish wax mine show a massive fat hump on the shoulders. Not merely an energy reserve, but a counterweight that provided additional force during hours of snow-shoveling with the horn.
Megaloceros giganteus — the giant deer — was proof of the steppe's richness. Its antlers spanned 3.6 meters, wider than a small car. Every year, males had to regrow 40 kilograms of antler. This required enormous amounts of calcium and phosphorus. Their mere existence was an indicator: only extremely fertile soil could feed such a wasteful animal. But when the steppe turned into dense forest, those magnificent antlers became a death trap. Between trees and branches, the deer got stuck — easy prey for cave lions. What had made them great, killed them.
There is a widespread misconception: mammoths died out because it got too warm. But mammoths had survived multiple warm periods before. Their killer was not the temperature. It was moisture. About 12,000 years ago, the ice sheets of the northern hemisphere began melting uncontrollably. The water poured into the oceans, altered ocean currents, and pushed massive amounts of moisture into the atmosphere. Over the mammoth steppe, which had always been dry and sunny, heavy clouds formed for the first time. It began to rain.
And worse still: the snow got deeper. For a mammoth, 20 centimeters of snow was a playground. But 2 meters of wet, heavy snow was a death sentence. The energy they needed to fight through the snow exceeded the energy the grass beneath could provide. The giants began to starve. At the same time, a new army marched from the south: trees. Shrubs, mosses, and the Siberian taiga invaded the steppe. Grass needs direct sunlight and dry soil. Trees blocked the light and altered the soil chemistry. Where golden grass once stood, dark coniferous forest now grew. The northern shrubs contained more toxins than grass. For bison and horses, they provided too few nutrients — and could even poison them.
When the grass vanished, the dominoes fell. Horses and bison starved or retreated to tiny remnant areas — where humans and cave lions made short work of them. Mammoths and rhinos were pushed into small, isolated populations on islands and in remote valleys. Inbreeding weakened their lineages. When the prey disappeared, so did the predators. Cave lions and saber-toothed cats were too large to survive on smaller prey in the dense forest. It was not a sudden death. It was a process spanning millennia, where every square meter of steppe was devoured by green forest. The last woolly mammoth died on Wrangel Island about 4,000 years ago. At the same time, the Egyptians were building their pyramids. It did not die from lack of water. It died because its empire had become a forest where it could no longer exist.
The mammoth steppe was not a passive ecosystem dependent on climate. It was a machine, powered by the feet of its own inhabitants. When moisture rose and the forest came, it lacked the engineers who could have defended it — because that very moisture killed the engineers first. Today, the steppe's legacy lies deep in the Siberian permafrost, where occasionally a perfectly preserved mammoth mummy surfaces. A message from a world that did not fall through strength. But through rain.
